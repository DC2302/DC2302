# Creative Brief: Flying Blind (OSO Operational Systems Optimization)

- **Goal:** lead-generation *(decided: the locked CTA is a direct consultation booking, and this brief pairs with "Sleepless Nights" as a two-ad lead-gen campaign for the same audience — consistency matters more than re-litigating the goal)*

- **Audience:** Blue-collar field-service business owners — oilfield/trades-style operations with trucks, crews, and job sites (same audience as "Sleepless Nights") — who run the operational side of the business on gut feel and memory because no one ever handed them a dashboard, and who suspect (but can't prove) that money and time are leaking out somewhere in the back office.

- **Core promise:** You're running a business with no instruments — no clear read on your real numbers, your workflows, or where things are breaking down — and OSO's job is to put gauges back in front of you, starting with a free 30-minute consultation.

- **Hook (first 2 seconds):**
  1. **[STRONGEST]** POV inside a truck cab / cockpit-style instrument cluster at night, dashboard gauges dark, cracked, or spinning wildly with no needle settling — then a hand grips the wheel tighter as the view fogs over. On-screen text: "Running your business with no gauges?" — visually distinct from "Sleepless Nights" (daytime/mechanical vs. that ad's bedside/night-interior), and the instrument-panel image *is* the "flying blind" metaphor made literal in the first frame.
  2. Extreme close-up on an oilfield pump-jack at dusk, silhouetted against fog/haze so thick the horizon disappears — camera can't find a clean line, mimicking zero-visibility. On-screen text: "You can't see three feet in front of you." — strong mood match to the pump-jack inspiration imagery, but more abstract/metaphorical than literal, slightly softer stop-the-scroll power than option 1.
  3. A stack of paper invoices and a calculator on a truck dashboard, owner's hand flipping through them while driving — grounded and relatable, but visually close to "busy back-office clutter" territory already implied by the prior ad; held in reserve, better suited as a beat-2 image than a hook.

- **Message arc:** *(4 beats → 4 blocks at 40s; final block doubles as CTA + reserved logo end-card per standing instruction)*
  1. **Hook (0-10s):** Dark, unreadable instrument panel / fogged-out sightline — the visceral feeling of running the business with no gauges to trust. On-screen text names it directly: "Flying blind?"
  2. **Diagnostic montage (10-20s):** Fast, matter-of-fact cuts naming the actual blind spots — don't have a real grasp on the numbers, back office not running optimized, workflows and processes that could clearly be tighter but no clear path to fix them. Confident and specific, not panicked — this is a diagnosis, not a crisis.
  3. **The turn — instruments come online (20-30s):** OSO Grizzly enters and the dark panel lights up — gauges snap into focus, needles settle, ice-blue readouts come alive. This is the visual payoff of the hook: OSO doesn't hand-hold, it restores visibility. Line: "OSO gives you the instruments back."
  4. **CTA + logo (30-40s):** Direct, confident close — "Stop guessing. Book a 30-minute consultation." Final seconds reserved for the OSO shield end-card (handled in post).

- **CTA:** "Book a 30-minute Consultation" *(locked by intake; matches the standing CTA established by "Sleepless Nights" — recommend both write back to `brand/brand-profile.md` Lead-generation row, currently TBD)*

- **Platforms:** TikTok, Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts — 9:16 vertical (primary; matches campaign partner ad and brand default aspect). No secondary platform in scope for this cut; a 16:9 LinkedIn re-edit could follow later but is out of scope here.

- **Length:** 40 seconds (4 blocks × 10s) *(locked by intake)* — matches "Sleepless Nights" runtime so the two campaign ads feel like a matched pair, and gives the diagnostic montage (numbers, back office, workflows/processes) its own full beat separate from the "instruments come online" payoff beat, rather than compressing both into one rushed block.

- **Tone notes:** Confident per brand voice, but here confidence reads as **diagnostic and assured** — a technician calmly telling you what's wrong and that it's fixable, not an emotional 3 AM confession. This is the deliberate complement to "Sleepless Nights": that ad was empathy-first and interior (bedroom, night, exhaustion); this one is instrument-first and slightly more external/procedural (cockpit, gauges, dusk fog, a system being restored). Minimal narration, let the dark-to-lit gauge payoff carry the emotional turn. OSO Grizzly should read as the calibrator/engineer presence — the one who flips the panel back on — consistent with its steady, competent characterization in the partner ad, just expressed through control and clarity rather than protective presence.

- **Proof points / facts used:** None — built entirely from the user's own framing ("flying blind," "don't have a grasp on your actual numbers," "back office not running in an optimized way," "room for improvement in streamlining... workflows and processes"). No statistics invented or sourced; all pain points are lived-experience relatability claims and should stay that way in execution.

- **Risks:**
  - The instrument-panel/cockpit metaphor must stay grounded in the trades/oilfield world (truck dashboard, equipment gauges) — if it drifts toward literal airplane-cockpit imagery it will feel off-brand and disconnected from the audience's actual work.
  - "Don't have a grasp on your actual numbers" and "not running in an optimized way" are diagnostic claims about the *viewer's* business, not OSO's results — keep language in "here's what we help you see" territory, not "here's what we guarantee we'll fix."
  - Running immediately after "Sleepless Nights" in-feed, audiences may notice the shared CTA/mascot/runtime — this is intentional (campaign cohesion) but the visual tone (day/dusk/mechanical vs. night/bedroom) must stay distinct enough that the two don't read as the same ad twice.
  - Depicting blue-collar/trades owners risks caricature if visuals lean stereotypical — reference imagery (truck cabs, pump-jacks, real gauges) should feel specific and respectful, not generic "hard hat" shorthand.
